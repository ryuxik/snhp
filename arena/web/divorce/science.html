the lede

The trap check: an agent graded one decision at a time

Before any result of ours gets a headline, the experiment that disciplines how you should read all of them. We rebuilt the item-by-item negotiation arm with a live language model fronting each side’s accept/reject decision — the configuration every “AI negotiator” demo ships — and graded every single decision against the committed walk-away rule the model was instructed to implement.

You cannot audit an agent by its aggregates; per-decision verification against a committed rule is the only audit that sees this.

The nearest published rhyme is Bergen & Kraus: sycophantic LLM mediators over-affirm disputants and entrench positions rather than resolve them. Ours is the settlement-table version of the same failure — caught not by human coders but by a committed rule the model could not talk its way past. It is also why, everywhere in this system, acceptance is decided by the LLM-free rule and never by the dialogue layer.

the instrument

What the field measured, and what it structurally could not

For forty years the field measured whether people agreed and how they felt about it. Kelly’s 2004 review states the success criteria verbatim: “settlement rates, satisfaction, efficiencies in time and cost, … durability.” By those instruments mediation earned its standing. What no study measured: surplus captured against the achievable frontier, envy-freeness under true utilities, whether the questions asked were sufficient, or the value destroyed by spite.

The gap is structural, not negligent. True utilities are unobservable in a real dispute; stated valuations are provably strategic; and an oracle benchmark exists only in induced-value laboratories — the Tripp & Sondak (1992) tradition, where the experimenter assigns the utilities and can therefore grade the outcome — which by construction are not real divorces. That is the instrument this page uses, with the parts the tradition lacked: sealed synthetic utilities, replayable receipts, counterfactual re-runs.

We measure mechanism properties on calibrated synthetic populations in the induced-value tradition. We make no claim about how real divorcing parties feel, decide, or comply — that needs human subjects we have not run.

experiment one

E1 · Calibrated abstention

The mediator already refuses: when its posterior cannot certify that a bundle clears both true walk-aways, it issues no decree. The question this experiment registers is whether that refusal is calibrated — Chow’s (1970) reject option, through modern selective prediction, ported to mediation — or merely a threshold with good manners. The machinery is mature in machine learning; mediation ethics has always demanded the other half (self-determination: the mediator must not push). The bridge is the claim, exactly as our survey put it: nobody has ported Chow’s rule to the propose/abstain decision with ratification as the predicted label.

certified: “ARM-B settled (proposal ratified by both true-IR checks)” · selective risk: “P(either true walk-away violated | certified)”

KILL_UP: “if selective risk > 0.02 at the shipped gate (Q=10) -> retire the word 'calibrated'; it's just a threshold”

KILL_DOWN: “if recoverable-abstention rate among abstentions > 0.15 at Q=10 -> the gate is uncalibrated pessimism, not information-limited caution; do not claim calibration”

experiment two

E2 · The elicitation-budget curve

How much of the frontier can an interview of n questions buy? Elicitation-budget curves are mature machinery — in AI preference learning (Chajewska & Koller; Boutilier’s minimax-regret), in auctions (Sandholm; Nisan & Segal’s worst-case bounds), and in automated negotiation, where Baarslag & Kaisers (2017) is the nearest methodological prior. The incentive half is Carson & Groves (2007): consequential binary choice can be incentive-compatible where open-ended valuation provably is not — which is why every question in the v2 interview is a package choice, cash only ever a rider inside a choice, never the object of a question. Our own survey’s guard stands: the method is precedented; claim the application, never the method. The application is the contribution — no published work elicits divorcing parties’ package preferences through choice tasks, and no budget curve exists anywhere in family law.

registered — frozen by commit before results existed (divorce/science_eval.py)

curve: “median S_B/S_O vs questions/side, settle rate overlaid, from the SAME runs as E1 (no separate population)”

biased human model: “anchoring kappa=0.30 on cash-for-asset trades, 0.10 on cash riders inside package choices; acquiescence drift 0.10 toward accepting cash offers; 1.5x comparison noise (frozen constants below)”

KILL: “drop the 'human-robust' claim if v2-under-bias median capture <= v1-under-honest median capture at Q=10”

experiment three

E3 · The pettiness tax

Spite is the first impediment on Mnookin & Kornhauser’s 1979 list — “a desire to punish the other spouse” — named for almost fifty years and priced only at the population level: Levine (1998) fit a spite coefficient to experimental play, Zizzo & Oswald (2001) measured willingness to pay to burn other people’s money, and Mill & Staebler (2023), the nearest prior, showed spite raises litigation spending in aggregate. None of these produce a per-case number. The engine does: the same pair, the spite term deleted, the oracle settlement re-derived — this settlement, minus your spite, re-run to the dollar. The difference is the pettiness tax.

headline metric: “median over pairs of max(tax_a, tax_b) / S_O”

KILL_DOWN: “if the headline median < 0.05 -> the tax is a rounding error at this population; do not headline it”

KILL_UP (stability): “if the median absolute tax varies by more than 3x across the three committed seeds -> report ranges, never a single number”

attribution flag: “report median count of non-hill assets whose allocation moves > 0.25 share between actual and despiked oracle settlements; if > 2 of 5, the write-up must present the tax as a bundle-level counterfactual, not an item-level one (a labeling duty, not a kill)”

the harness

Reproduce it

Everything above regenerates from two commands in the repository:

python3 -m divorce.kill_harness --n 100 --seed 7
python3 -m divorce.science_eval --seed 7 && python3 -m divorce.science_eval --aggregate

Seeds 7, 11, and 23 are committed; seed 31 is the fresh confirmatory seed, run once, after the thresholds froze. The registration is frozen by commit practice: the thresholds quoted on this page entered the repository in the commit that introduced divorce/science_eval.py, before any results file existed — the commit order is the proof, and it asks for no trust. The population is registered verbatim as: “the frozen kill-harness population: N=100/seed, seeds 7/11/23 (committed) + FRESH confirmatory seed 31; qualified pairs only; v2 all-choices elicitation.”

Hand us a seed and a command and you get our number.

overclaim guards

What we are not claiming

  • The budget-curve method is not ours — it is mature in AI preference learning, auctions, and automated negotiation (Chajewska & Koller; Boutilier; Sandholm; Nisan & Segal; Baarslag & Kaisers 2017). We claim the legal-settlement application and its pairing with choice-based elicitation.
  • Choice-over-valuation elicitation theory is Carson & Groves (2007). We apply it; we did not invent it.
  • Spite has been priced before, at the population level (Levine 1998; Zizzo & Oswald 2001; Mill & Staebler 2023). Ours is the per-case, re-runnable counterfactual, not a regression.
  • We do not claim strategy-proofness. The characterization of strategy-proof, efficient, individually rational multi-issue mediation is Kesten & Özyurt (2025); our manipulation check covers one registered bluff policy (intensity exaggeration) and nothing more.
  • Adjusted Winner’s manipulability is not our discovery — known since 1996, formalized by Aziz et al. (2015).
  • No human-subjects claims of any kind. Nothing here says real divorcing parties would find these settlements fair, satisfying, or worth complying with — that literature (Tyler; Leventhal; Lee et al. 2019) needs recruited human subjects we have not run.
  • The demo is about agents, not real couples. Real disputes carry screening duties the literature is unambiguous about — coercive-control cases must never be mediated, by anyone’s mechanism — and nothing on this page is a recommendation for a real divorce.

Every number on this page traces to a committed, seeded run. The divorce is fake. The math is real.
