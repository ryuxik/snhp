# B6 — Network synergies: what adopters unlock TOGETHER

*Design + pre-registrations, 2026-07-10. The wholesale result (cross-venue
coordination = 85–97% of the wholesaler's gain; produce = 100%) says the
block, not the venue, is the unit of economics. This phase makes that true
on the consumer side too.*

## A. Cross-venue bundles (the Uber Eats layer)

1. **Slack-swap bundles.** Venues' dead hours interlock: the bodega peaks
   at lunch, boba dies 3–4pm, the bar dies 3–6pm, the barber midmorning.
   A cross-venue bundle sells one venue's PEAK TRAFFIC into another's
   SLACK: "bodega sandwich + a 3:30 boba slot, −$1.40." Mechanically a
   trilateral Nash bundle (consumer + venue A + venue B) with an
   inter-merchant transfer — and the wholesale finding predicts the shape:
   *price is the grease* — the referral transfer is what makes operational
   slack-swaps feasible. Pre-registered: bundle arm at the block level;
   prediction — the gain concentrates in slack-hour venues' margins with
   the traffic-source venue paid via transfer, net-positive for both plus
   the consumer (three-way surplus split), echoing H-W1's window result.
2. **Itinerary bundles.** Friday: barber slot + happy-hour table + a
   bouquet — composed from slack capacity across three venues, priced as
   one negotiated cart. The broker composes itineraries the way Uber Eats
   composes restaurant + courier.
3. **The culturally-existing wedge:** parking validation. "Park 2 hours,
   validated with any boutique purchase" is a cross-venue bundle merchants
   already understand — our version just computes it (parking's slack ×
   fashion's conversion lift). First bundle to ship.
4. **Clearing.** Inter-merchant transfers need settlement and audit — this
   is the June AP2 cart-mandate + registry stack becoming the block's
   CLEARING HOUSE. The attestation network stops being a discount tier and
   becomes the rails for merchant-to-merchant value flows (fee: bps on
   cleared transfers). This is the Visa position, made concrete.

## B. Information sharing between businesses — with the guardrail FIRST

**The guardrail (non-negotiable, stated before any feature):** price
coordination between competitors is collusion — the algorithmic-pricing
literature and live litigation (RealPage) draw the line clearly. The rule
we adopt: adopters may share DEMAND-STATE telemetry (foot traffic, weather
shocks, arrival curves) and OPERATIONAL state (delivery windows, capacity)
— never price intentions, margins, or quote data between substitutes.
Complements may coordinate bundles; substitutes may not see each other's
pricing signals, period. Pre-registered collusion audit: any shared-
telemetry arm must show consumer surplus NON-DECREASING vs private-
telemetry; if sharing raises consumer prices, the feature dies.

1. **The shared block posterior.** The day's demand shock is COMMON across
   the street — one venue's 8am arrivals inform everyone's mult_hat. A
   block-level Gamma–Poisson posterior pooled across adopters: strictly
   better forecasts, faster censoring-escalator convergence, useless to
   non-adopters. THE network effect with increasing returns: each adopter
   improves every other adopter's pricing. Pre-registered: shared vs
   private posterior arms; prediction — forecast error falls with adopter
   count, profit per adopter RISES with adopter count (the adoption-curve
   sim then shows whether the block converges to full adoption).
2. **Portable reliability.** Kept-appointment/no-show history as an
   attested, consented, portable score across slot venues: reliable
   customers get deposits waived block-wide. Consumer-visible benefit of
   the registry — verification buyers want, again.
3. **Upstream flow.** Venue sell-through shared (consented) with
   wholesalers → route and stock planning → better terms: vendor-managed-
   inventory economics riding the wholesale tier we already proved.

## C. Consumer leverage — resident clusters

1. **The buyer club with a demand commitment.** The block's regulars
   aggregate into a negotiating cluster: "44 residents commit to ~$3.1k/mo
   across bodega+boba+bakery." The deep insight our framework can PRICE:
   commitment reduces the venue's demand VARIANCE, and variance is exactly
   what the min-gain buffer and safety stock cost — so group rates are not
   charity, they're purchased forecast certainty. Pre-registered:
   committed-cluster arm; prediction — cluster members pay measurably below
   spot-negotiated AND venue profit rises via smaller buffers and pars
   (both-sides win from commitment: the wholesale windows result, consumer
   edition).
2. **The building as the natural cluster.** NYC co-ops/rentals negotiate
   as one: resident parking off-peak, standing bakery orders, the
   building's own attested profile. Distribution wedge: one board vote
   signs 200 households.
3. **Surplus pooling.** End-of-day bakery/flower surplus sold as a pooled
   neighborhood buy (negotiated group-buy of the perishable tail — Too
   Good To Go economics, but computed and cleared on our rails).
4. **The inversion that ties the whole thesis:** merchants' core problem
   is not knowing their demand curve (the miscalibration channel — our
   headline result). A resident cluster's verified aggregate disclosures
   ARE that demand curve. Clusters can therefore SELL calibration:
   standing discounts in exchange for consented aggregate demand data.
   The information rent returns to the people who generate the
   information, priced by the same broker. Pre-registered: calibration-
   for-discount arm; prediction — venue σ_cal effectively shrinks, total
   surplus grows, and the split favors consumers in proportion to cluster
   size.
