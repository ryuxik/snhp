§ 1 — The week the market turned

Two-thirds off, and nobody's celebrating

On June 24, the cheapest ticket to Spain–Belgium in Los Angeles cost $4,740. This morning it costs $1,317 — down 68%. France–Morocco in Boston: down 66%. Norway–England in Miami: down 57%. In the three days after the United States lost 4–1 to Belgium and joined Mexico and Canada on the way out, average quarterfinal get-in prices halved, and the number of tickets listed for resale nearly doubled, from 28,285 to 49,415. Someone, presumably as commentary, listed an England–Norway quarterfinal ticket for $8 million.

This is the first World Cup priced like an airline. FIFA adopted dynamic pricing — a tournament first — and raised prices on more than 90 of the 104 matches between October and April, by about 34% on average across the main categories. The cheapest seat at the final went from $6,730 at launch to $8,680 by December to $10,990 by April, and in April FIFA invented a new "Front Category" at $32,970 — reassigning some fans who had already bought Category 1 to worse seats in the process. That last move is part of why the attorneys general of New York and New Jersey subpoenaed FIFA on May 27. FIFA's president, asked about all of this in June, told fans to "just chill, relax," and said the average ticket costs below $500 — a claim the AP noted didn't hold for list prices.

Here's the uncomfortable part, and the reason this piece exists: by FIFA's own objective, the system worked. The group stage sold 99.7% of capacity — 4.64 million tickets, an all-time record. Prices that rose into the boom are now falling into the bust, exactly as a revenue-managed market should. The outrage and the efficiency are both real. What's left for the people still holding tickets, or still wanting one, is a pricing problem — and pricing problems can be solved.

§ 2 — The model

The final is a lottery with four draws left

Eight teams remain (one quarterfinal spot — Switzerland or Colombia — was still being decided as this published). The bracket: France–Morocco and Spain–Belgium feed the Dallas semifinal on July 14; Norway–England and Argentina–QF4 feed Atlanta on July 15. The final is July 19 at MetLife. So today's $9,346 get-in is really a price on a probability distribution over twenty possible finals, weighted by the bookmakers' odds — FanDuel makes France (~53% to reach the final) and Argentina (~45%) the modal pairing, a 2022 rematch.

The model prices each version of July 19 with a demand multiplier per finalist — England's traveling support, the enormous Argentine and Colombian communities around New York, France and Spain's global draw — and an elasticity calibrated to the sharpest fact in this week's data: while quarterfinal prices fell 57–68% on the co-hosts' exit, the final fell only about 20%. A final's demand is anchored to the event, not the matchup. The Atlanta semifinal (the England/Argentina side) already trades 17% above Dallas ($2,708 vs $2,320) despite Dallas having France and Spain — the market is already paying for the marquee bracket. Every multiplier is modeled and swept; §7 owns that honestly.

§ 5 — The 30% wedge

Why 49,415 tickets are for sale and so few are selling

Every resale on FIFA's official marketplace pays FIFA twice: 15% from the seller, 15% from the buyer. For a trade to happen, a buyer must value the ticket at roughly 1.35× what the seller will accept. In a rising market that's friction; in a crashing one it's a wall — sellers who bought at April's dynamic-pricing peaks anchor on what they paid, buyers anchor on this week's collapse, and the wedge swallows the space between them. That's the mechanism behind the ballooning listings: the market isn't clearing, it's queuing.

One more engine note, connecting this to piece No. 3: run the final's marginal seat through the same dynamic-pricing solver that priced Erik Lira's transfer window, and the profit-maximizing ask starts near $12,800 and marks down toward July 19 — the exact shape FIFA's own market followed when it quietly cut prices across all 104 matches in early June, and the shape the resale market is following now, two weeks at a time. Whether it's a Bundesliga winger or Section 348 Row 20, a wasting asset obeys one law: the schedule only ever goes down, and the deadline sets the pace.

§ 6 — What this doesn't say

Does the model tell the whole truth?

The team demand multipliers are modeled, not measured — the sweep moves every scenario band, and the Atlanta-vs-Dallas semifinal premium is the only direct market evidence for them. The bookmaker odds carry vig (normalized per bracket side, but still bookmaker beliefs, not truth). The wedge calibration assumes lognormal buyer values and cost-anchored sellers; the ~1-in-5 killed trades and the 42% revenue peak are illustrative of the mechanism, not audit findings. And a real buyer faces frictions no model prices: FIFA's transfer app failed publicly in late June, stranding resale buyers mid-purchase, and the FBI is warning about fake ticket sites. The model tells you what the lottery is worth. Getting the ticket delivered is your own adventure.

§ 7 — Predictions

Graded on July 15 and July 19 — the fastest scorecard in this series

  • If England or Argentina reaches the final (market: 73%), the get-in lands $9,200–$11,800 by July 16. If both marquee sides deliver — a France/Spain vs England/Argentina final — the top of that band.
  • If neither reaches it, the get-in drops below $8,400 in every remaining scenario, and below $6,000 if the final involves Belgium, Norway or Switzerland.
  • Resale listings keep growing faster than sales until the semis resolve. The wedge model says a falling market with a 30% fee queues instead of clearing — watch the listing counts, not the prices.
  • The last 72 hours before kickoff will see markdowns, not markups, in every scenario except the marquee-vs-marquee final — the same deadline law as every piece in this series.

A market can work perfectly and still work against you. The difference is whose objective it maximizes — FIFA wrote its own, and now you can read yours.
