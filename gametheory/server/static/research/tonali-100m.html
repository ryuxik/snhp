§ 1 — The deal, as reported

What actually happened

Tottenham and Newcastle agreed a club-record £100m package for Sandro Tonali on July 2; the move was completed on July 6. The facts on the record, from Sky Sports and ESPN reporting:

  • Structure£92.5m guaranteed, plus £7.5m in add-ons tied to European qualification
  • PathSpurs' ~£75m opener was rejected on June 20; Newcastle also reportedly turned down ~£90m before the £100m package landed
  • SellerNewcastle were willing sellers — a reported £45m profit on the £55m paid in 2023, with Tonali under contract to 2029 — who privately believe the full £100m is "very likely" to be achieved (Sky)
  • BuyerSpurs narrowly avoided relegation last season, hired Roberto De Zerbi, and had already spent £85m on Mateus Fernandes
  • BackdropManchester City interest in Tonali (a ~€110m bid was reportedly being prepared); the window runs to September 1 — terms were agreed 61 days early

Notice the tension in that third bullet. Newcastle believe the Europe add-on will "very likely" pay out — they're booking it at close to face value. Spurs, a team that just flirted with relegation, are underwriting a bonus that only costs them money in the world where they've succeeded — a world where European revenue covers it. Both sides think the conditional £7.5m is a good trade at the same time. That's not a contradiction. That's the deal working exactly the way bargaining theory says it should. The rest of this piece makes that precise.

§ 2 — The model

Four issues, two beliefs, one pie

A transfer negotiation isn't one number. I model the Tonali deal as a package over four issues — guaranteed fee (£80–100m), Europe-conditional add-on (£0–15m), payment schedule (upfront, two, or four instalments), and sell-on clause (0–15%) — 315 possible packages in all. Each club values a package in cash-equivalent £m, and the two valuations differ in exactly the places that make trades possible:

Every belief parameter above is my assumption — that's why §6 sweeps all of them across 10,000 draws rather than defending any single number. The machinery is the SNHP negotiation engine: the same Pareto-frontier, Nash-bargaining, and Bayesian-inference code that powers its agent-to-agent commerce tools, pointed at a transfer instead of a SaaS contract. Nothing football-specific was added to the math.

§ 3 — The frontier

The add-on is the logroll — but it should have been bigger

Plot every feasible package by the surplus it gives each club and the efficient deals trace the Pareto frontier — packages where neither club can do better without the other doing worse. The structure the clubs actually chose sits close to that frontier, and its distinctive feature — the conditional add-on — is exactly what the frontier is made of. Because £1 of Europe-conditional money is worth ~£0.70 to Newcastle but costs Spurs only ~£0.40, every conditional pound manufactures ~£0.30 of joint surplus out of pure belief disagreement.

Read the aqua point carefully, because it's the headline. The engine's Nash solution isn't a different split — it's a different shape. Cut the guarantee by £7.5m, double the conditional money to £15m, pay the guarantee upfront: Newcastle end up exactly as well off as they actually did, and Spurs keep an extra £2.5m of expected value. At base beliefs, the clubs stopped one round of structural creativity short. The direction of the actual deal was right; the dose was low.

The model also predicts a dog that didn't bark: no sell-on clause. Because a sell-on costs the buyer more than it's worth to the seller here (low resale odds, high flexibility penalty), it's jointly inefficient, and the frontier zeroes it out almost everywhere. The reported deal has no sell-on. The engine wouldn't have written one either.

§ 4 — The split

Newcastle won the room

Efficiency says how big the pie is; the split says who ate it. To locate the split, I replayed the negotiation from Spurs' real £75m opener with the engine playing both seats — each side running the same concession-and-inference machinery against the other, single-issue, on the guaranteed fee.

Two symmetric, equally-patient negotiators land at £90.6m flat. Newcastle actually banked £92.5m guaranteed plus an add-on the model says they value near £5m — the model puts their haul at £97.8m of expected value, roughly £7m above the symmetric equilibrium. Across the Monte Carlo, Newcastle's median share of the realized joint surplus is 61%. The seller won, and not by a rounding error.

Why? Patience — and look at where it actually came from. Not primarily Manchester City: Tonali wanted De Zerbi, which quietly hollows a rival bid as a threat. Newcastle's patience was the contract to 2029 — a seller who doesn't have to sell discounts the future slowly, and rejecting ~£90m mid-window is what that looks like in the wild. Spurs, rebuilding off a 17th-place finish, discount it fast. In bargaining terms, Newcastle at δ ≈ 0.93 per round, Spurs at δ ≈ 0.76 — and in the Rubinstein equilibrium (the standard model of offer-counteroffer bargaining, in which the more patient side simply gets more), that patience gap alone hands the seller roughly three-quarters of the £24m bargaining zone (76.5% with Spurs proposing first, as they did), implying a total around £100.4m. One more real-world force the model books silently under "ceiling": Newcastle were selling inside the league, to a direct competitor — and Premier League clubs charge rivals a premium a foreign buyer wouldn't pay. The actual deal sits between the symmetric replay (£90.6m) and the full patience-asymmetry solution (~£100.4m) — which is where you'd expect it, since City's interest was real but reportedly never reached a formal bid. One reported detail lands eerily close: Newcastle reportedly turned down an intermediate ~£90m bid — almost exactly what a symmetric negotiation is worth. They refused the equal-leverage price, and got paid for the difference.

§ 5 — Stress test

10,000 versions of this negotiation

Every belief number in §2 is an assumption, so the honest move is to stop defending point estimates and sweep them: 10,000 draws over Newcastle's add-on confidence (0.50–0.85), Spurs' effective add-on cost (0.30–0.65), both reservations (±£6m), and the schedule premiums. For each draw, recompute the full frontier and ask two questions — was the conditional structure right, and how much joint surplus did the actual deal leave unclaimed?

Three results survive the sweep:

The conditional add-on pays both clubs at once in 9 of every 10 simulated worlds. Whenever Newcastle's confidence exceeds Spurs' effective cost — which is what the public record suggests — conditional money beats guaranteed money for both clubs simultaneously. This is the robust finding, and it's structural: it holds wherever the reported belief asymmetry does, and doesn't depend on any point estimate.

The deal consistently undershoots. Median £2.1m of joint surplus unclaimed, and in a linear model the frontier always wants the add-on maxed at £15m. Reality is concave — bigger add-ons invite conditionality disputes and measurement fights — so treat "£15m" as directional, not literal. But "bigger than £7.5m" holds across nearly every draw.

Newcastle beat Nash. The Nash-bargaining guaranteed fee is £85m at the median draw (£80–90m across the 10–90th percentile). Newcastle got £92.5m. Whatever Spurs' negotiators told their board, the symmetric-fairness benchmark says they paid a patience premium of roughly £5–10m.

§ 6 — What this doesn't say

Does the model tell the whole truth?

The fees are journalism, not documents. "£92.5m + £7.5m" is well-sourced reporting, but installment schedules and add-on conditions beyond "European qualification" aren't public. The schedule dimension in the model is assumption end to end.

The utilities are modeled, not measured — and the split leans hardest on one of them. No one outside the two boardrooms knows either club's true reservation or ceiling. The "who won" arithmetic is most sensitive to Spurs' modeled £106m ceiling: at £100m Newcastle's share reads 88%, at £112m it reads 52%. "Newcastle ahead" survives the whole range; the 61% is the base-case reading, not a measurement. A club running this internally would replace these priors with its own numbers — which is rather the point.

Linear surplus, one-shot deal. The model ignores relationship effects (these clubs will trade again), agent fees and player-side terms (Tonali's £275k+/week was its own negotiation), and treats add-on £s as linearly divisible. And the engine's head-to-head validation comes from commerce negotiations, not transfers — this piece is a demonstration of method, not a backtest.

§ 7 — Why this matters

The negotiation is a solvable layer

Football analytics has spent fifteen years pricing players. Almost nobody prices the negotiation — yet the gap between a well-structured and a lazily-structured deal on a single £100m transfer was worth £2–4m of joint value here, and the split moved £7m+ on patience alone. Those are recruitment-department-salary numbers, recovered not by scouting better but by structuring better.

Everything in this piece ran on general-purpose bargaining machinery with football supplied as data. A club running it for real would hold enormous advantages over this outside view: it knows its own reservation, its actual cash constraints, its true belief about the add-on triggering. The interesting question isn't whether the model is right about Tonali. It's what the same machinery returns when someone who knows the real numbers feeds them in — before the call with the other sporting director, not after.

That's the experiment I want to run next. If you work on the inside of these deals and the framing here is wrong somewhere, I'd genuinely like to know where — that's the fastest way this line of work gets better.
