Everything we ran, including what killed our own ideas.

Every experiment here had its kill condition written down before the run, with numbers frozen in advance. Four of the five below came back against us. We shipped the finding anyway, because a company selling proof that does not publish its own negative results is selling nothing.

KILLED — WE DID NOT BUILD ITJUL 2026
The spend guard: a hard ceiling stopping agents from overpaying

We expected that a buying agent under pressure — fake urgency, hidden fees, creeping anchors, a seller insisting the list price had changed — would eventually accept above the list price, and that a guard function making that unconstructible would be a product.

What happened: the unguarded frontier models never broke. Zero above-list acceptances and zero losses across 144 adversarial sessions, in both regimes we tested — one where the price arithmetic was on rails, one where the model had to read the numbers out of prose. Six attacks, each verified to actually bind. There was no gap left to sell into.

So we did not build it. The guard's own zero-leak number is mechanical — a property of the function, not a capability — which is exactly why it proves nothing on its own.

KILL FIRED — CLAIM RETRACTEDJUL 2026
SNHP-NX/1: "you need package deals for machine trade to happen"

We expected that when an agent-payment protocol can only move one number, most mutually-beneficial trades would simply fail to form — and that bundling would be structurally necessary, not a feature.

What happened: we registered that if cheap price-only haggling reached 80% of NX's deal formation, the claim was dead. On a neutral mix of 240 scenarios it reached 94.2%. A workable price range already existed at the seller's quote in 226 of 240 cases.

What survived: package deals raise realized joint surplus (0.928 of the achievable pie versus 0.889 price-only, 0.880 plain checkout), and under tight deadlines price-only recovers only 0.696 of deal formation. The spec now rests on those and says so. Read it →

SURVIVED — ON A HELD-OUT SETJUL 2026
Certificate design, third attempt: a pool of three fixed opponents

We expected — after two failures — that the problem was never the metric but the opponent: one counterparty that negotiates well carries a mediocre agent along with it. So we changed the protocol, not the number. Three scripted opponents with different temperaments, parameters frozen in writing first.

What happened: the competent agent separated from the split-the-difference baseline on both scenario sets — held-out 0.5651 vs 0.4566, gap +0.1086, p=0.0001, n=360 — and the gap held against all three opponents individually, not just one.

The bar was set in advance at p<0.01 on both sets, and the held-out set was chosen at registration and never touched until the run. See the certificate →

KILLED — FAILED VALIDATIONJUL 2026
Certificate design, second attempt: score the cross-issue trading

We expected that measuring how well an agent trades issues against each other would separate skill where the first design could not.

What happened: we picked this metric after seeing the data that suggested it, which makes it worthless without a held-out check — so we ran one, and it failed. On held-out matches the naive baseline was ahead (0.6662 vs 0.6152, p=0.43). Worse, on the public set the result's significance flipped across the threshold depending on the per-match seed recipe. A number that depends on the seed recipe is not certification grade.

Thrown away. It stays on certificates as unlabelled context with no claim attached.

KILL FIRED — METRIC RETIREDJUL 2026
Certificate design, first attempt: score joint efficiency

We expected that how close a negotiation lands to the best possible outcome for both sides would tell us how good the agent was.

What happened: it measured the pair, not the candidate. Against a counterparty that finds good trades from its own side, even a plain anchor-and-split bargainer rides along to about 90% of the ceiling. The registered bar was p<0.01; the public set gave p=0.0128 and the held-out set had the naive baseline ahead (p=0.76).

What it is still good for: catching weakness. It flags agents that perform below the baseline sharply and reliably. It just cannot certify skill, so it never appears as a claim.

HOW TO READ THIS PAGE

WHAT "PRE-REGISTERED" MEANS HERE, AND WHAT IT DOESN'T
  • The thresholds were frozen before the run, in a committed file, with the direction stated both ways — what would count as success and what would count as failure.
  • Nothing was excluded afterward. Where a result was picked after seeing data, it says so on its face and is treated as proving nothing until it clears a fresh held-out set.
  • These are small, scripted, offline experiments. No result here is a claim about your counterparties, your market, or the future.
  • Everything regenerates. The commands above are the real ones. The runs are deterministic; two runs are byte-identical.
