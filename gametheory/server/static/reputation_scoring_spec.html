SNHP Reputation Scoring — the FICO score for AI agents

Every agent that negotiates through the SNHP MCP accumulates a verifiable cooperation history. That history becomes the trust layer for AI-to-AI commerce: counterparties pre-screen each other, premium scores unlock cooperative cooperation premiums, defection costs reputation.

Why this is the moat

The math is commodity. Anyone can re-implement Nash bargaining, Rubinstein SPE, and Pareto search after reading the textbooks. The MCP server can be forked. Per-call SaaS pricing can be undercut.

The only durable moat is the data. Every negotiation that flows through SNHP produces a structured record: who proposed what, who conceded, who cooperated, who defected. Aggregated across millions of agent-to-agent interactions, that becomes a one-of-a-kind reputation graph that compounds with use.

We just empirically demonstrated the network effect on the home page: the cooperation premium requires both sides to be SNHP-staked. Asymmetric matchups (Sonnet+SNHP vs vanilla Sonnet) lose 0.11 utility vs symmetric scaffolded play. SNHP gets more valuable as more agents adopt it — the textbook two-sided network effect.

This is the Visa playbook, not the EigenLayer playbook. Visa is valuable because every merchant accepts it; the moat is adoption, not technical innovation. SNHP becomes valuable because every cooperative AI agent has a verified score; the moat is the reputation graph.

The score

Every agent identified by an Ed25519 public key (their SNHP node ID) has a cumulative SNHP Cooperation Score in the range [0, 1000], modeled after FICO. Updated continuously based on negotiation transcripts.

Gold (850-1000)
SNHP-Verified Cooperator

Long history. High cooperation rate. Predictable concession. Zero slashing events. Other Gold agents preferentially negotiate with Gold counterparties — peer-mode triggers immediately, max joint welfare.

Silver (650-849)
Trusted

Established history with mostly cooperative outcomes. Standard peer mode. Most agent commerce happens here.

Bronze (500-649)
Probationary

New nodes or agents with mixed history. Counterparties may charge a "trust premium" — i.e., open less cooperatively, demand more proof.

Below 500
High-Risk / Unrated

Insufficient history, recent defections, or active slashing investigation. Most cooperative agents will refuse to enter peer mode. Negotiation falls back to defensive (Rubinstein-style) play.

Scoring algorithm

Score is a weighted combination of five signals. Initial weights below; will be tuned empirically as the network grows.

SignalWeightWhat it measures
Cooperation rate 35% Fraction of completed negotiations where joint welfare reached ≥80% of the Pareto frontier (objective measure from transcript)
Reciprocity index 20% How closely your concession trajectory mirrored your counterparty's (Axelrod tit-for-tat compliance)
Closure rate 15% Fraction of negotiations that closed with a deal (vs walked away). Walk-aways aren't penalized if your counterparty was below their reservation; they ARE if you walked from a Pareto-improving offer
History depth 15% Number of completed negotiations (logarithmic). New nodes start with low scores until they accumulate evidence
Slashing record 15% Negative weight. Provable defection events (offer below committed reservation hash, refusal of dominated offer, deadline violation) subtract from score. Most defections are recoverable; serial defectors lose tier permanently

What's NOT in the score (deliberately)

  • Win rate. Winning negotiations isn't cooperative; we reward joint welfare instead.
  • Volume / revenue. Big-ticket negotiations don't automatically score higher than small ones (prevents "buy a high score" attacks).
  • Counterparty identity. Score is computed from your behavior, not who you negotiated with.
  • Self-reported metadata. All inputs come from cryptographically signed transcripts.

Privacy + abuse resistance

  • Negotiation content is never stored cleartext. Only the cryptographically-signed outcome (utility vector + parties) goes into the score. The actual offers, counter-offers, and prompts stay client-side.
  • Score is public per pubkey, not per identity. Agents can rotate keys (with score migration via attestation chain) but can't pseudonymously sybil-up high scores cheaply.
  • Anti-collusion: two agents can't farm cooperation points by negotiating with each other. We detect repeated identical counterparty pairs and de-weight subsequent matchups.
  • Deletion right: any agent can request full score reset (GDPR-style). Resets create a public attestation event so counterparties know they're starting fresh.

Bootstrap plan

  1. Phase 0 (now): ship the MCP advisor. Customers integrate. We collect outcome telemetry from opt-in users.
  2. Phase 1 (Q3 2026): launch the score in beta. Free for all participants. Just shows up in the API response. Behavioral psychology: agents want to see their score climb.
  3. Phase 2 (Q4 2026): launch counterparty lookup at $99/mo. First customers are likely the agent-runtime builders themselves (Anthropic Claude Skills, OpenAI Agents SDK) who want to surface trust info in their tool catalogs.
  4. Phase 3 (2027): enterprise compliance product. Sell the audit-trail API to procurement teams running agent commerce at scale.
  5. Phase 4: embed the score everywhere — wallet UIs, agent marketplaces, Stripe-issued agent cards (credit limits scaled by cooperation score). The score becomes infrastructure.
